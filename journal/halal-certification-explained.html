What Does 'Halal Certified' Actually Mean?

TL;DR

Halal certification means: meat sourced from approved slaughterhouses where animals are killed with a specific blessing and method; no pork or alcohol on the premises; separate utensils where needed; auditing by a recognized halal certification authority. Basha is fully halal-certified.

More than just 'no pork'

A common misconception is that 'halal' just means no pork or alcohol. The reality is more involved. Halal certification covers the entire supply chain: animal welfare, the slaughter method itself (zabihah — a swift, specific cut while the animal faces Mecca, accompanied by a blessing), the absence of haram (forbidden) ingredients in seasonings and oils, and the separation of certified and non-certified items in storage and preparation.

The certification process

A restaurant becomes halal-certified through an authorised halal certification body (in Canada, this is typically the Halal Monitoring Authority or similar bodies). The process includes: supplier audits to verify all meat suppliers are themselves halal-certified, on-site inspections of the kitchen and storage, ingredient-level review of all seasonings and sauces, periodic re-audits to maintain certification.

How Basha maintains halal

Every protein we serve — beef, chicken, lamb — comes from certified halal suppliers with full chain-of-custody documentation. Our spice blends, sauces, and oils are all halal-verified. We do not serve pork. We do not serve alcohol. Our fryers and grills are dedicated halal — there is no shared cookware with non-halal items. We re-verify our certification annually.

Why it matters to non-Muslim customers too

Halal certification represents a high bar for sourcing transparency. It means the meat traces back to a specific farm, slaughtered under specific conditions, with a paper trail. For customers who care about food sourcing — Muslim or not — halal-certified often correlates with smaller-scale, more traceable supply chains than industrial alternatives.
