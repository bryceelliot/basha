Donair vs Shawarma vs Gyro — What's the Difference?

TL;DR

Donair is spiced ground beef (Canadian, from Halifax 1970s). Shawarma is marinated sliced chicken or beef (Levantine origin). Gyro is Greek lamb-beef. All three use a vertical rotating spit. Basha serves donair and shawarma in Kelowna.

Same technique, different tradition

All three dishes — donair, shawarma, and gyro — use the same cooking technique: meat stacked on a vertical spit, slow-roasted as it rotates, with thin slices shaved off as they cook. That technique came out of the Levant (modern Lebanon, Syria, Turkey) in the 19th century. Different countries adapted it with their own meats, marinades, and breads.

Donair — the Canadian invention

The donair as we know it today was invented in Halifax, Nova Scotia in the early 1970s by a Greek-Canadian named Peter Gamoulakos. He took the shawarma technique, adapted it to North American tastes by using spiced ground beef (rather than sliced meat), and paired it with a sweet condensed-milk sauce that became iconic in the Maritimes. The Canadian donair has spread west, with regional variations along the way. Western Canadian donair shops — including Basha — typically use a savoury garlic sauce instead of the Maritime sweet sauce.

Shawarma — the original

Shawarma is the oldest of the three. Originating in the Levant, it uses thin slices of marinated meat (chicken or beef most commonly, sometimes lamb) stacked on the spit. The marinade is the centerpiece — chicken shawarma typically uses cumin, coriander, paprika, turmeric, garlic, and lemon. Beef shawarma adds cinnamon, allspice, and black pepper. The meat is shaved off as the outside crisps, giving every order a different blend of textures.

Gyro — the Greek interpretation

Gyro (pronounced 'YEER-oh') is the Greek version. Traditional gyro uses lamb or a lamb-beef mix, seasoned with oregano, thyme, marjoram, and onion. It's served in pita with tzatziki (a yogurt-cucumber-garlic sauce), tomatoes, and onions. Gyro tends to be lighter than donair and more aromatic than shawarma.

How to choose

If you like dense, savoury, hearty meat with garlic — order donair. If you like marinated, spice-forward, slightly tangy meat — order shawarma (chicken for lighter, beef for deeper). If you like herb-and-yogurt and lamb-forward profiles — that's gyro territory. At Basha we don't do gyro, but we do both donair and shawarma — try the mixed shawarma wrap to taste both shawarma styles in one order.
