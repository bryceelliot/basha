How Halifax Accidentally Invented Canadian Donair

TL;DR

Peter Gamoulakos opened a restaurant in Halifax in 1973 selling Greek gyro with lamb. Halifax customers found it too gamey. He switched to spiced ground beef and a sweet condensed-milk sauce. The Canadian donair was born.

1971 — Halifax, broke and stuck

Peter Gamoulakos arrived in Halifax in 1971 from Greece. He'd worked in restaurants in Athens. Halifax in the 1970s was a maritime port city with deep working-class roots and zero exposure to Mediterranean cuisine. Peter opened a small restaurant called Velos and tried to sell traditional Greek gyro — lamb on a vertical spit, served with tzatziki in pita.

Halifax customers said no

The lamb was too strong. The yogurt-cucumber tzatziki was unfamiliar. The pita was unlike the bread Halifax was used to. Velos was struggling. Peter realized that what worked in Athens wouldn't translate to Halifax without changes.

1973 — the accidental invention

Peter rebuilt the dish. He swapped lamb for spiced ground beef (more familiar to North Americans). He swapped tzatziki for a sweet sauce made from condensed milk, vinegar, sugar, and garlic — a flavour profile closer to what Halifax knew from desserts and dairy. He kept the vertical-spit technique and the warm pita. He renamed it 'donair' — a phonetic spelling of 'doner' that was easier for English speakers.

1980s — the spread

By the late 70s, Velos was famous for its donair. Other Halifax restaurants copied the recipe. By the 1980s, donair shops opened in every Maritime city. Halifax declared donair its 'official food' in 2015. Maritimes universities exported the donair west: Calgary, Edmonton, Vancouver, the Okanagan, all developed their own donair scenes through the 90s and 2000s.

The West Coast variation

Western Canadian donair shops mostly dropped the sweet sauce. The savoury garlic sauce (toum, in the Lebanese tradition) became the West Coast default. The meat got slightly less sweet, the spice profile a bit deeper. The Halifax-style sweet donair is still revered as the original; the West Coast version is what most BC and Alberta customers expect today. At Basha we serve the West Coast style — savoury garlic, deeper spice, fresh-carved off the spit.
