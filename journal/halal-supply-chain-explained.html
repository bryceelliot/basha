How Halal Meat Actually Gets to Your Plate

TL;DR

True halal certification means: certified farm → certified abattoir → certified processor → certified storage → certified kitchen. Every step audited. If one link fails, the whole chain is broken.

Step 1 — the farm

Halal certification starts before slaughter. Animals must be raised in conditions that meet specific welfare standards — adequate space, clean water, no stress. Some halal authorities go further and require specific feed (no animal byproducts, no genetically modified crops in some interpretations).

Step 2 — the abattoir

The slaughter itself follows zabiha rules: a swift cut to the throat with a sharp blade, the animal facing Mecca, accompanied by a blessing (bismillah, Allahu akbar). The animal must be alive at the time of slaughter. Stunning is permitted in some halal jurisdictions if it doesn't cause death, but many strict authorities reject any pre-stun. The abattoir is audited by a halal certification body — typically the Halal Monitoring Authority in Canada — that verifies each batch.

Step 3 — processing + storage

After slaughter, halal meat must be kept separate from non-halal meat. Different cutting boards, different freezers, different transport vehicles. Cross-contamination breaks certification. The processing facility is audited periodically; facilities that pass get to keep the certification logo.

Step 4 — the kitchen

When the meat reaches a halal restaurant, the chain has to continue. We can't store our halal proteins next to non-halal items. Our fryer oil for the falafel is dedicated halal — no shared cooking surfaces. We document supplier chain-of-custody for every protein. We're audited annually.

Why the cheap 'halal' label often isn't

Some restaurants advertise halal but fail the supply chain. They might source from a halal-certified abattoir but then store and prep the meat alongside non-halal items, breaking the chain. Truly halal means the chain is unbroken from farm to plate. That's the standard at Basha.
