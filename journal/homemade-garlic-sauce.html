Why Our Garlic Sauce Tastes Different (And How to Make Yours Better)

TL;DR

Toum is whipped raw garlic, lemon, salt, and oil — emulsified slowly. The real Lebanese version takes 15 minutes and is what makes our donair garlic sauce taste different from every store-bought brand.

It's called toum

The Lebanese white garlic sauce that comes with every shawarma in the Levant has a name: toum (طوم). Not 'aioli' (that has eggs), not 'mayo,' not 'tzatziki' (Greek, with yogurt). Toum is just garlic, lemon juice, salt, and neutral oil — emulsified slowly until it turns into a fluffy white spread that holds its shape.

The technique that ruins it

Most North American donair shops cheat. They start with mayonnaise, add garlic powder, call it garlic sauce, and serve it. The result is heavy, oily, and tastes like garlic-flavoured cream. Real toum is light, sharp, almost meringue-like in texture — and it tastes overwhelmingly of fresh garlic, not garlic powder.

Our recipe

Two heads of fresh garlic peeled, salt, lemon juice, and 4 cups of grapeseed oil. Garlic + salt go in a food processor first, run for 60 seconds. Then alternate: 1 tablespoon oil, 1 teaspoon lemon juice, 30 seconds blend. Repeat slowly until all the oil is incorporated. The mixture starts watery, then suddenly emulsifies into a thick white cloud. That's toum. Refrigerate for at least an hour before serving.

Why it matters for donair

On a beef donair, you don't want the sauce competing with the spice. The savoury garlic profile of toum cuts through the richness of the meat without adding fat, sugar, or heaviness. That's why the West Coast donair tradition (savoury garlic) tends to outperform the Maritime tradition (sweet condensed-milk sauce) for customers who don't grow up with the Halifax style.
